How this works — and what it can't do

Plain-English methodology. If anything here sounds like a guarantee of profit, re-read it — it isn't.

1. Where the prices come from

Live decimal odds for every World Cup match from The Odds API, covering the Irish-accessible bookmakers (Paddy Power, Betfair, Bet365, BoyleSports, Ladbrokes, William Hill). The timestamp in the header is the truth about freshness. Prediction-market prices (Kalshi, Polymarket) are pulled alongside as an independent cross-check.

2. "Fair" probability — de-vigging

Bookmaker odds include a built-in margin (the "vig"): the implied probabilities of Home/Draw/Away sum to more than 100%. We remove it proportionally for each bookmaker, then average across books to get a consensus fair probability for each outcome. Two honest limits:

  • "Fair" means the market average, not the truth. Beating it means you got a better price than the consensus — it does not prove the consensus is right.
  • Proportional de-vigging slightly flatters longshots (the favourite–longshot bias). Small edges on big prices are therefore treated as noise and labelled speculative, not value.

3. Edge and EV

Edge = fair probability − the probability implied by the best price (in percentage points). It's a model diagnostic. The money number is EV (expected value): EV = fair probability × price − 1. "EV +€0.30 per €10" means that if this exact bet were repeated many times and the fair probability is correct, you'd average +€0.30 profit per €10 staked. Any single bet still just wins or loses — EV only shows up over dozens of bets. Variance dominates small samples.

4. The prediction-market cross-check

Kalshi and Polymarket are real-money exchanges with low margins. Their prices are normalised to sum to 100% and compared against the book consensus. When they disagree with the books in your favour, that's confirmation; when they disagree against you, the "edge" is probably the model being wrong, and the card says so.

5. The AI analyst

A language-model analyst writes a football read for each match using only sourced inputs: the official FIFA squad lists, FIFA rankings, sourced recent form, one tournament-wide injury digest from a web search, and venue conditions (live forecast inside 16 days, otherwise climate normals — always labelled which). It is explicitly forbidden from inventing stats, records, managers or formations, and it never sees enough to judge value — it supplies football reasons; the price layer judges the odds. It is a second opinion, not a fact, and it can be wrong.

6. Best Bets — the two gates

A bet appears only when (1) the analyst explicitly recommended that outcome for football reasons, and (2) the current price is fair or better for that pick. Verdicts: Bet Now (EV ≥ +2% and outside the longshot-noise band), Lean (roughly fair price), Track Price (analyst likes it, price short), Avoid at Price.

7. Accumulators are −EV. Full stop.

Multiplying legs multiplies the bookmaker's margin. The acca tools here exist because people enjoy accas: they find the least-bad version (single-book pricing, high-probability legs, honest EV in red). The long-term expectation of every acca on this site is negative and the cards say exactly how negative. The genuine edge, where it exists, is in singles.

8. What would prove this works

A logged record. The My Bets journal records the model's fair probability and EV at the moment you log a bet, so over time you can compare expected P/L with actual P/L and check whether prices you took beat the market's later prices (closing line value). Until that record exists over a meaningful sample, treat every number here as a hypothesis, not a promise.

9. Bankroll

Flat, small stakes — 1–2% of a bankroll you can afford to lose — survive variance. Chasing losses and increasing stakes after wins are both how positive-EV bettors go broke. If betting stops being fun, stop.
